# 明, personal brand brief

Draft. Built to react to and argue with, not to lock in yet.

## The character

明 (míng) is built from two pieces: 日 (sun) and 月 (moon). Two different light sources, one character. That structure is the spine of this brand, not just a nice fact about the word.

## What 明 means for Ming Rui

Five angles, in her own words, with the sun/moon idea folded into the leadership one since that's where it fits most naturally.

1. Illuminating paths. To solutions, to what's possible, to the next step when no one else can see it yet.
2. Making the complex visible. Taking something tangled and showing people the actual shape of it.
3. Energetic. The kind of presence that moves a room forward.
4. Empowering teammate and leader, as sun and moon. Sometimes she's the sun: the energy that pushes a team through inertia, generates its own heat, hard to ignore. Sometimes she's the moon: steady, reflective, lighting the room without needing to be the source of it. Same person, two different ways of giving light depending on what the room needs that day.
5. Light to your darkness. The moon side again: this is specifically the light that only matters because it's dark out. Shows up clearest when things are hardest, not when things are already easy.

Decided: the sun and moon framing runs under all five angles, not just #4. As a map: 1 and 3 are the sun (the active push, the energy that starts things moving), 4 and 5 are the moon (the steady, reflective presence, the light that counts most in hard moments), and 2, making the complex visible, is what happens when both are present at once. Worth carrying this split through the actual copywriting once we get there, not just stating it once in an about page.

## Brand personality (draft, react to this)

Clear-eyed. Energizing without being loud. Direct. Generous with attention. Steady under pressure rather than performing calm. Not precious about her own ideas.

## Positioning statement (tentative)

"Energy when something needs to move. Steadiness when something needs to hold."

Landed here after rejecting the original three drafts as too cheesy, and after testing both a literal sun/moon version and an adjective version (warm/cool). This is the plain, no-metaphor version of the sun/moon duality: two short declarative sentences, no light or bright language, the duality carried by the parallel structure rather than by naming sun or moon directly.

## Copywriting direction

Voice principles:

- Lead with what she saw or figured out, not with adjectives about herself. "Energetic" and "empowering" are things other people conclude after reading what she did, not words she puts on herself in the first sentence.
- Short, declarative sentences. The brand is about clarity, so the writing has to demonstrate it, not just claim it.
- When describing impact, name the specific fog she cut through and what became visible after. That's the literal action of 明, so it should show up as a recurring move in the copy, not just a logo concept.
- No light/bright metaphors stacked on top of each other in the same paragraph. One per page is plenty. If every sentence says "illuminate," "shine," or "clarity," it stops meaning anything.

Word bank:

Use: clear, visible, untangle, the shape of the problem, steady, direct, what changed, what I'd do differently, illuminate, brighten, and their synonyms, passionate.

Avoid: spearhead, leverage, drove alignment, visionary, game-changer, any sentence that could appear in a company's annual report.

Before and after, applying this to her current About draft:

Before: "I'm Mingz, a UX Designer and Researcher. I've spent the last 5 years working in cross-functional teams across banking and government-tech, untangling complex problems and designing solutions that actually work."

After (draft, not final): "I spend most of my time in rooms where the problem is tangled and nobody fully agrees on its shape yet, banking and government-tech, mostly. My job in those rooms is to get everyone looking at the same picture. Five years in, that's still the part I'm best at."

The "after" isn't precious, it's just to show the shift: fewer label-words (Designer and Researcher), more of the actual moment (a room, a disagreement, her specific job in it).

## Visual direction, three distinct options

Your current site (cream, ink, vermillion) is a Notion-style palette. None of these three assume you keep it. Pick a lane, or tell me what's wrong with all three and we'll find a fourth.

Option A, Daylight. Sun-forward. Warm whites, one strong warm accent (could keep vermillion or push toward a true gold/amber), high contrast, generous whitespace. Reads as: confident, energetic, a little bold. Risk: closest to what you already have, might not feel like a deliberate evolution.

Option B, Lunar. Moon-forward. Cooler near-black or deep ink background with a single bright, precise accent (could be a pale gold or a cool white) used sparingly, like the moon being the only light source in a dark frame. Reads as: steady, confident, slightly more senior and restrained. Risk: cooler palettes can tip into "generic dark mode tech site" if the accent isn't distinct enough.

Option C, Duality. Both registers exist on the site, not as a literal day/night toggle gimmick, but structurally: maybe the home/about pages run warm and energetic (sun, her own voice) and the case study pages run cooler and more restrained (moon, the work itself, letting the problem be the subject). Reads as: the most conceptually tight to 明, but the highest execution risk, two systems have to feel like one brand, not two.

My instinct is Option C is the most senior-feeling idea but the hardest to pull off well, and Option B is the safer bet if you want something sharp without the risk of it feeling split. Worth sitting with reference sites in each direction before deciding.

## Type and motif ideas

- A typeface pairing where the body text is plain and quiet (the "moon," reflecting the content, not competing with it) and one element, maybe just numerals or section labels, gets a slightly warmer or bolder treatment (the "sun," the place where her voice or energy shows up directly).
- A recurring motif of "before/after" or "fog to clarity" in how case studies open, visually echoing the brand idea instead of just stating it.
- Avoid literal sun/moon iconography (actual sun or moon icons) unless you want the brand to read as cute rather than considered. The idea should live in structure and contrast, not in clip art.

## Mark or wordmark ideas

- A wordmark that's just "Ming" or "明" set in two weights or two tones in the same word, literal sun/moon as typographic contrast rather than as imagery.
- A simple mark built from negative space, where a circle (sun) and a crescent (moon) overlap to form a third shape. Only worth pursuing if you want an actual icon/favicon, not just a wordmark.
- Simplest option: no separate mark at all, just a confident wordmark treatment of her name, with the 明 concept living in the writing and the light/dark structure rather than in a logo. Worth considering given the brief itself says the work should speak for the thinking, not a clever icon.

## Decided so far

Visual direction: Option A, Daylight.

Palette: no changes. Vermillion stays, forest green stays.

Positioning statement: "Energy when something needs to move. Steadiness when something needs to hold." (tentative.)

Duality scope: runs under all five angles, not just the leadership one.
