This was a project done in my 3rd year of college for my Design Thinking module. Snack Chat will always stay in my portfolio because it is the project that piqued my interest and propelled me to pursue User Experience Design and Design Thinking as a professional career.

I’m leaving it raw and untouched (and kinda documenting style) in just the way how I wrote it back in 2018 to remind myself, and you, the reader, that we all start from somewhere 😊

## Overview

The theme for this module's assignment revolves around the needs and pain points of students in higher education. I teamed up with two exchange students from Sweden and Netherlands. After a meeting sharing and discussing the difference on the outlook towards life between students in Singapore and Europe, we narrowed down on the theme and decided on the central idea of "Passion".

Because Singaporeans seem to be in real lack of passion.

## Approach

### Part I

#### Stage I: Empathise - Interviews at North Spine

Our first task in the design thinking process is to empathise with our target group, which is students. We conducted interviews with different students from different courses around NTU about how they find their time in school and we asked them about their future dreams. We had three different levels of questions and we started with the first level that consisted of easier questions just to make the persons that were interviewed more comfortable. These were our questions:

<aside>

***LEVEL 1***

*Describe your day at school*

*What and why are you studying in school?*

*After school activities? Why are you doing these activities? How do you feel about it?*

***LEVEL 2***

*Enjoying what you are doing, or not?*

*Why are you going to school?*

*How do you feel about the education system here? (from primary to tertiary education) What about the grading system?*

*Why did you choose this course?*

*What do you want to do after you graduate?*

*Are you are learning enough in class?*

***LEVEL 3***

*What are your dreams?*

*Why would you think that the younger generation should be concerned about the future?*

*What do you think education could do to ease the problem?*

</aside>

#### Stage 2: Define – Definition of problems around passion

We gained interesting answers and insights from the interviews about passion. Initially, the interviews were a big challenge for us because of the fact that **most of them were not able to define their passion**. The most usual reactions we got as soon as we mentioned the word ”passion” was *”UHHH”*. However, the interviewees became more and more comfortable the more we spoke about their hobbies and passions.

One thing that most of the discussions/interviews had in common was most of them ended up in talking about **pressure from society and parents**. This caught our attention, however, during the first round of ideation, we chose to elaborate on the idea of combining passion with finding a job.

### Stage 3 & 4: Ideate and prototype – Dream Catcher (Job/forum portal)

Our most potential idea from the first round of ideations was ‘dream catcher’, a portal that not only helps you find your passion, but also helps to connect other people with the same interests in a forum.

However, the feedback in class made us realize that the idea was neither original nor anything new. We decided to dive back to stage 1 to find more insights of our target group.

### Part II

#### Stage 1: Empathise – Survey about passion and parents

After an internal discussion, we realized that at any point, the path of following your passion can be stopped or obstructed by parents. Because the finding that **parents play an important role in the decision making** of the student stood out, we decided to focus on the role of parents in following and finding passion. Therefore, we conducted a survey that asks students what role their parents play in their future decisions. We received 106 responses from students in NTU from a variety of courses.

This became clearer to us when we gained more insights about the communication between the students and their parents. It was obvious that there was a lack of communication because of the fact that almost **60% never or rarely spoke about their dreams and goals with their parent** *(Figure 7)*.

This indicates that there is a **barrier between students and parents** to talk about their passion and future dreams. An explanation for this is the **lack of understanding** between two generations, which cause friction in the relationship between parent and child.

Among the reactions, many students indicated that their parents would not understand anyway, which is why they refrain from talking to their parents about their dreams and passions.

#### Stage 2: Define – New problem statement based on passion and parents

image-fig 8

At the end of the survey, we asked if they wished that they had a chance to discuss the subject with their parents and almost 65% said yes *(Figure 8)* which also made it easier for us to come up with a solution for the problem statement that we propose. Our analysis let us to the following problem statement: parents and children need to find a way to communicate and learn more about each other, preferably without having to bear with the awkwardness of baring their hearts out in person.

#### Stage 3: Ideate – What Lah, SIMS for parents, drinking game, food game

Our survey let us to the idea that improving and encouraging communication between students and parents is necessary to start the conversation about passion. One of our thoughts during the ideation process was a chat, inspired by the ”matchmaking” application Tinder. We chose this idea over other ideas such as SIMS for parents, drinking game and food game, because it was most potential for overcoming the conversation barrier.

#### Stage 4: Prototyping & Feedback

image-fig 9

The central idea of the app is based on matching children with other parents that have the same interests that they would like to talk about. We named the chat ‘What Lah’, as Singaporean parents and children often say ‘What lah’ when responding to each others questions. The app targets Singaporean youths above 18 and their parents. How it works is simple: parents/children sign up with their IC, they both fill in a personal profile, the algorithm will match parents and children based on their interests, and then the chatting starts. Parents will get insider knowledge of other children and children will get insights of other parents about their issues. In addition, we added a second element to the app: ‘Ask Lah!’, which is a flipcard game that presents questions based on the subject you get from the fortune wheel. This element was developed for families who are ready to talk and play a game together.

After some feedback sessions with the class, we noticed that it is maybe not the best idea to match adults with children of a young age, as that could create risks for people with ill-intentions trying to get in touch with children for inappropriate reasons rather than discussing passions. In addition, the chat also did not truly encourage the parents and children to talk with each other, since they only talked to other children and parents. **There was no personal contact between the parents and their own children**, which made the app miss the mark in its goal to ‘start the conversation’.  However, we could build further on the concept of ‘Ask Lah!’, since the question game concept supported the idea of fostering conversations between parents and children. Some insights that shaped our future idea:

- Something tangible and personal suits the older generation more than something digital.
- Overcoming the barrier does not mean students should avoid the confrontation with their parents. Instead, students should get together with their parents and rediscover their love for each other

#### Stage 3 & 4: Ideate and prototype – Snackchat

We had a stressful overnight ideation session because our submission deadline was closing in, and we still had to produce and test our solution. The fear of failing again definitely was huge but we still gave our all... and came up with our final baby: Snackchat

We realized that we needed to make it personal, simple and real. Hence, came up with the idea of ‘Snackchat’, which is a question based game inside a bag of crisps. The idea is inspired by the notion of coming together as a family while sharing food. Singaporean parents would often come together with children to watch TV and share a bag of snacks, or to eat food together in general. Sharing food also carries the idea of the children wanting to take care of the parent by bringing them food, as the child should initiate playing the game. As mentioned before, Snackchat is simple: each person takes one crisp per round. Every crisp has a little paper with a question tied to it. The question will be available in English, Chinese, Malay and Tamil to make it accessible for all Singaporean families. There are three levels of questions: easy, medium, hard. The easy and medium questions will warm up the players to answer the more difficult questions. The difficult questions will address ‘heavy’ subjects as dreams, goals, passions, professions, school, etc. The bag of crisps will come in one flavour and will cost around $2-$3.

Before putting in effort to design the packaging for it, we did two rounds of testing:

##### 1. Guerilla Testing around campus

image 

image

image 

image

We went to North Spine to test the idea on groups. We managed to test the idea on a total of 8 groups. Overall, the feedback was very positive. 7/8 groups would feel comfortable to ask the questions to their parents/for their parents to ask them these questions. When we asked the groups if they would buy the snack in the supermarket amongst all other snacks, all groups agreed that they would buy the Snackbag game, especially for a gathering/party and if its tasty and cheap (within $2–$3). 50% of those whom we tested the game on also pointed out it will definitely help the family members to open up to each other.

Moreover, we also got valuable feedback. The questions that were put inside the bag are too complicated. Many groups pointed out that they prefer mostly ‘laidback and easy’ questions in the bag. Some groups preferred different bags with different levels of questions, from serious to easy questions.

The feedback in class was also unexpectedly very positive. Many classmates pointed out it is a fun game, especially used in a drinking game. Of course we did not want the purpose of Snackchat to be solely fun, which is why we revised our questions and reformulated some.

##### 2. Testing with real families

image

We tested the game with 5 families (including mine). 

Through this game, my parents and I were able to be more comfortable to speak about topics we rarely speak about during our daily conversations. We all got to know each other better at the end of the game, and I got to know certain emotions or thoughts that my parents might be having but they were not comfortable to let me know. 

However, we also got some feedback from my parents about the game. Families that are too conservative would not appreciate some of the questions. It is also harder for conservative families to play the game in a comfortable manner, since the gap between children and parent is harder to bridge in those families. We continued improving our questions by taking some of the inappropriate ones out.

We then went on to test the game with the remaining 4 families and asked them to answer a survey after playing it.

image

After playing the game, all respondents described a positive feeling (e.g. ‘re-energised, warm, happier, comfortable’. Both students and families enjoyed the game, as it allowed them to discover more about the other.

In terms of Snackchat’s main goal, starting the conversation about various personal topics, it scores and average of 8.

In addition, 100% of the respondents agreed the game brought the family closer together.

The overall feedback seemed very positive, however there are some improvements that we can work on:

– More lighthearted questions

– More activities in questions (showing videos, guessing other person’s question)

### How we came up with the questions, and gave them varying levels of (answering) difficulty

We did our research on how to **manage conflict** in a group/team/family situation and found many resources on the internet. All of them were similar and the most concise source was from [judyringer.com](https://www.judyringer.com/resources/articles/we-have-to-talk-a-stepbystep-checklist-for-difficult-conversations.php).

There are 4 steps to a successful outcome.

#### Step #1: Inquiry

> “Cultivate an attitude of discovery and curiosity. Pretend you don’t know anything (you really don’t), and **try to learn as much as possible about your opponent/partner and his point of view**. Pretend you’re entertaining a visitor from another planet, and find out how things look on that planet, how certain events affect the other person, and what the values and priorities are there.”
> 

#### Step #2: Acknowledgment

> “Acknowledgment means showing that you’ve heard and understood. Try to understand the other person so well you can make his argument for him. Guess at his hopes and **honor his position**.”
> 

#### Step #3: Advocacy

> “When you sense your opponent/partner has expressed all his energy on the topic, it’s your turn. What can you see from your perspective that he’s missed? Help clarify your position without minimizing his. “
> 

#### Step #4: Problem-Solving

> “Ask your opponent/partner what he thinks might work. Whatever he says, find something you like and build on it. If the conversation becomes adversarial, go back to inquiry. **Asking for the other’s point of view usually creates safety and encourages him to engage.** “
> 

Taking the learnings from Judy Ringer, we structured the “easy” level questions to be mainly the “getting-to-know-you-better” type of questions, like what Step #1 suggested. And from the way the family members would react to the answers given by each player, we would have achieved Step #2. Family members could derive a better idea of each other through tweaking Step #3 a little bit, by seeing from their own perspective something the other person currently *needs* in his/her life, or is *seeking for* in his/her life, instead of something he/she has missed.

Using the learnings from Step #3, family members could choose to take it a step further by going on to Step #4 to solve unseen, hidden problems happening in the family that may be unravelled through playing *Snackchat*.

We also focused our questions to centre them more around the topics of: *School, Work, Dreams, Wellbeing, Family* and our main theme, *Passion*. The questions are structured in a way that the answers for the questions would help the family members playing the game know more about each other’s perspectives on these topics that I listed above.

## Conclusion and Reflections

Through our tests with families, it is confirmed that the game exactly hits the mark in terms of bringing families closer together, fostering conversations and raising important subjects related to passion. The quality of the questions are improved in our final product, as well as the variety of flavours and the format of the questions. The whole journey, from Dreamcatcher, What Lah to Snackchat, lead us to an end product that is simple, delicious, fast and fun. A product that Singaporeans cannot resist because of the fun and quizzical twists.

This was one of the most satisfying projects I have worked on as I was able to follow through an end-to-end process from doing user research, to testing and iterating our solution multiple times. Our ideas were backed by data, and it felt good to have statistics and numbers to fall on when we were justifying our solution. I also enjoyed every moment I had interacting with my users and getting very useful feedback. This experience made me discover that I could actually fuse my top three biggest passions into a career: Talking to people, Design, Problem Solving. 

And lastly, sometimes technology is not the answer to a problem. We have to disconnect, to connect.
